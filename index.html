Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS): A Beginner-Friendly Guide

A visual introduction for students who are just starting in molecular biology and bioinformatics. This mini handbook explains what NGS is, how it works, what files it produces, and how to think about sequencing data with confidence.

1. Foundations

What is NGS?

Sequencing means reading the order of nucleotides in DNA or RNA. Traditional sequencing usually reads one fragment at a time, while NGS reads enormous numbers of fragments in parallel.

A

What sequencing means

DNA stores biological information as a sequence of bases: A, T, C, and G. Sequencing tells us that order.

B

Why it is called next-generation

These methods improved scale dramatically by reading many fragments at the same time instead of reading only a few.

C

Why it is powerful

NGS lowers cost per base and makes genome-wide analysis possible in both research and applied biology.

What this means

NGS is not simply a faster machine. It changes the scale of biology.

Why this matters

Large biological questions often cannot be answered one fragment at a time.

Student takeaway

Choose sequencing methods according to the question, not only the buzzword.

2. From sample to answer

The Big Picture Workflow

NGS is a connected chain of laboratory and computational steps. Each step affects the quality and meaning of the final result.

SC

1. Sample collection

The biological material is collected, such as blood, tissue, cells, or an environmental sample.

Why this step matters: poor sampling can introduce degradation or contamination before sequencing even begins.

EX

2. DNA or RNA extraction

Nucleic acids are purified from the sample so they can be measured and processed.

Why this step matters: impure or degraded material causes weak downstream results.

QC

3. Input quality control

Concentration, purity, and integrity are checked before library preparation.

Why this step matters: poor starting material often leads to poor libraries.

LP

4. Library preparation

DNA or cDNA fragments are converted into machine-readable molecules by adding adapters and indexes.

Why this step matters: most sequencers cannot read untreated genomic material directly.

SR

5. Sequencing run

The instrument reads bases from library fragments and records them as digital signal.

Why this step matters: this is where molecules become computational data.

FQ

6. Raw reads

The output is usually a FASTQ file containing sequences and base quality scores.

Why this step matters: FASTQ is the starting point for most analyses.

RQ

7. Read quality check

Software examines quality trends, adapter contamination, duplication, and other issues.

Why this step matters: not every read is equally trustworthy.

AL

8. Alignment or assembly

Reads are matched to a reference genome or assembled into longer sequences.

Why this step matters: genomic context is needed before many questions can be answered.

VC

9. Variant calling or quantification

Depending on the experiment, software detects mutations, counts transcripts, or profiles taxa.

Why this step matters: this step turns aligned data into measurable results.

BI

10. Biological interpretation

Results are linked back to disease, function, ecology, evolution, or the original research question.

Why this step matters: data becomes useful only when interpreted in context.

3. Major use cases

Types of NGS applications

Different NGS experiments answer different questions. The right method depends on whether you care about whole genomes, coding regions, RNA, microbes, or a small group of known targets.

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)

What it studies: almost the entire genome.

When it is used: broad mutation discovery and genome-wide analysis.

What data it gives: reads and variants across the genome.

Example: finding resistance mutations across a bacterial genome.

Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)

What it studies: protein-coding regions.

When it is used: rare disease studies and many clinical variant projects.

What data it gives: variants in captured exonic regions.

Example: searching for coding mutations linked to an inherited condition.

RNA-Seq

What it studies: RNA molecules and gene expression.

When it is used: transcriptomics and expression comparison.

What data it gives: transcript counts and expression patterns.

Example: comparing gene expression in tumor versus normal tissue.

16S rRNA sequencing

What it studies: bacterial community composition using a marker gene.

When it is used: microbiome surveys.

What data it gives: relative abundance of bacterial taxa.

Example: comparing gut microbiota between groups.

Metagenomics

What it studies: all DNA in a mixed community.

When it is used: complex environmental or host-associated microbiomes.

What data it gives: taxonomic and functional information.

Example: analyzing all microbial DNA in soil or wastewater.

Targeted sequencing

What it studies: selected genes or hotspot regions.

When it is used: focused diagnostics or known candidate genes.

What data it gives: deep coverage over chosen targets.

Example: screening a focused cancer panel.

Small RNA or microRNA sequencing

What it studies: short regulatory RNAs.

When it is used: biomarker or regulation studies.

What data it gives: counts of small RNA species.

Example: detecting microRNAs linked to inflammation or cancer.

4. Vocabulary

Important terms every student should know

NGS becomes much easier once common words in laboratory protocols, file names, reports, and analysis outputs stop feeling unfamiliar.

This quick filter makes the glossary easier to review before class, lab work, or bioinformatics practice.

Read

A sequence produced by the machine from one library fragment.

Read length

The number of bases in each read, such as 75 bp or 150 bp.

Depth / coverage

How many times a position is read. Higher coverage often improves confidence.

Reference genome

A known sequence used as a guide when aligning reads.

Alignment

The process of placing reads onto a reference genome.

FASTQ

A file that stores read sequences and quality scores.

BAM

A compressed binary format of aligned reads.

VCF

A file format that stores called variants such as SNPs and indels.

Adapter

A short artificial sequence added during library preparation.

Library

The prepared collection of fragments that is ready for sequencing.

Paired-end reads

Two reads generated from opposite ends of the same fragment.

Single-end reads

One read generated from only one end of each fragment.

Variant / SNP

A difference from the reference sequence. A SNP is a one-base change.

QC / mapping rate

QC means quality control. Mapping rate is the fraction of reads that align.

6. Trust the data carefully

Quality control and why it matters

Raw reads are not automatically ready for interpretation. Some are strong, some are weak, and some contain technical artifacts such as adapters or contamination.

Why reads are not ready immediately

The sequencer can produce uncertainty in base calling, technical sequences, duplicates, and other artifacts that need inspection before analysis.

What low quality means

A low-quality base is a base call the instrument is less confident about, which increases the chance of mapping or variant errors.

Q

Base quality scores

These estimate the confidence of each base call. Higher scores mean lower error probability.

A

Adapters

If inserts are short, the read may continue into adapter sequence, which is usually removed.

D

Duplicate reads

Too many duplicates may reflect PCR bias or low library complexity rather than new information.

How a beginner can read a QC report

Start with a few questions: Are base qualities generally high? Is there adapter contamination? Are duplication levels suspicious? Do reads map well to the expected reference? You do not need to master every plot at once.

7. Wet-lab logic

Library preparation explained simply

Library preparation converts biological molecules into a form the sequencing machine can recognize. This step often feels mysterious at first, but it becomes much easier when broken into a few clear actions.

What this means

The library is a prepared version of the sample, not the untouched original DNA.

Why this matters

Many artifacts begin during library preparation, including PCR duplicates and uneven representation.

Student takeaway

Library quality strongly influences read quality, duplication, and downstream confidence.

8. Sequencing platforms

How sequencing machines read DNA

Different platforms use different chemistries, but the main idea is the same: the instrument detects signals corresponding to nucleotide identities and converts those signals into digital sequence data.

The first concept beginners should understand

Some platforms usually produce short reads and some produce long reads. Short reads are often highly accurate and cost-effective. Long reads are especially helpful when the goal involves repeats, structural variants, or long molecules.

IL

Illumina in simple terms

Illumina commonly uses sequencing-by-synthesis. Bases are added step by step, and the machine records a signal after each cycle to infer the sequence.

ON

Oxford Nanopore in simple terms

A molecule passes through a tiny pore, and changes in electrical signal help identify the sequence in real time.

PB

PacBio in simple terms

PacBio observes DNA synthesis directly as a polymerase works on a single molecule, helping generate long-read information.

9. After the machine finishes

Basic bioinformatics after sequencing

The machine creates data, but bioinformatics turns that data into information. The exact pipeline changes by experiment, but the main logic is similar across many workflows.

FASTQ generation and QC

The sequencing output is commonly delivered as FASTQ files, then checked for base quality, adapters, duplication, and other warning signs.

Trimming

Low-quality tails and adapter sequences may be removed before alignment or assembly so later steps work more reliably.

Alignment or assembly

If a good reference exists, reads are aligned to it. If not, sequences may be assembled into longer contigs or transcripts.

Counting, calling, visualization, interpretation

RNA-Seq often counts transcripts, while DNA sequencing often focuses on variants. The last step is explaining what the results mean biologically.

What this means

Sequencing and analysis are connected but not identical tasks.

Why this matters

Similar read files may need very different pipelines depending on the experiment.

Student takeaway

Bioinformatics is not extra. It is part of the experiment itself.

10. Common pitfalls

Common mistakes and misconceptions in learning NGS

Beginners often focus on read numbers or the machine alone. A stronger way to think is to connect experiment design, data quality, and the biological question.

High read count does not always mean good data

Reality: many reads can still be low quality, duplicated, contaminated, or poorly matched to the question.

More coverage is not always useful

Reality: useful coverage depends on the goal. Very high depth may add cost more than insight.

QC is not optional

Reality: skipping QC can turn technical artifacts into false biological conclusions.

Different experiments require different pipelines

Reality: RNA-Seq, WGS, 16S, and metagenomics are not analyzed in the same way.

Sequencing and interpretation are not the same thing

Reality: a sequence file is data, not a conclusion. Biological context is still needed.

11. Practical decision-making

How to choose the right NGS approach

One of the most useful beginner skills is matching the experiment to the question. The question should come first, followed by the assay and then the analysis plan.

12. Final memory map

What students should remember about NGS

If you remember the ideas below, you already have a strong beginner-level foundation for future study in genomics, molecular diagnostics, microbiology, and bioinformatics.

NGS reads many fragments in parallel

That parallelism is what makes large-scale sequencing practical.

The experiment starts before the machine

Sample quality and library preparation strongly shape the data.

QC protects interpretation

Never trust a dataset fully until you understand its quality profile.

Different questions need different NGS methods

WGS, WES, RNA-Seq, 16S, and metagenomics are not interchangeable.

Bioinformatics is part of the experiment

Sequencing files become useful only after careful analysis and interpretation.

Student takeaway

NGS is best learned as a connected story: sample, library, machine, reads, QC, analysis, and biological meaning. Once that story feels clear, the terminology and technical details become much easier to understand.
